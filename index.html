Introduction

In Winter of 2014, the brave young men and women of the Data Science Student Society at UCSD entered the Yelp Dataset Challenge in order to witness how the era of Big Data impacts the business decisions of professional social review services like Yelp! In our data analysis, we determine the difficulty in predicting user's review stars given the reviews they left as well as provide our best classification model, and we also add in a few visualizations for fun.

Data Wrangling

The first thing we noticed was the size of the Reviews table which made loading the data difficult, so we divided it into quarters. Then we sampled the first quarter of the Reviews and inner-joined it with the key-ids from Tips, Users, and Businesses tables and creating the TURBO subset with 37K records (Tips, Users, Reviews, Businesses, Only). We later on use the Checkins table in order to find the top businesses in our similarity matrix visualization.

Exploratory Data Analysis

In starting our analysis, we were initially surprised to see that the review distributions in our subset were skewed to the 4 and 5 star categories heavily. They consist of around 80% of the distribution, whereas the 1, 2, and 3 star categories are each only around 10-15% at most.

This is confirmed by a separate analysis by Max Woolf on 1 and 5 star reviews which showed, excellent visualization aside, that Yelp reviews have started to appear more optimistically biased as time passes. Our sample subset reflects this skewed distribution, and uneven class distribution will become noteworthy further on in our predictive analytics task.

Next we were curious about what kinds of words, including unigrams, bigrams and trigrams, are characteristic of different star categories so we threw in some quick wordcloud visualizations using the R "tm" and "wordcloud" packages.

In EDA, we're trying to find pertinent features for the next step which is the predictive analytics/machine learning task, so we did factor analysis on the review text (e.g. average token length between star classes, positive/negative words rate per review, average star given by friends of reviewers, etc). We noticed after preprocessing the review text by stemming, removing stopwords, and tokenizing, the average token length is around 20-30 tokens for each star classes, and the token lengths mainly range between 0 and 150.

Later on in the predictive analytics task we attempted to improve our classification accuracy and we returned to feature engineering and finding correlations between factors, but we will include it here under the EDA section for organization. We correlated nearly all of the given base factors from each table except for business attributes and review dates, and included new factors like average friend stars and negative/positive word ratios.


Most of the upper triangle of the correlation matrix have higher values of course because they come from the same user table, but of particular interest to us is the bottom row on the yaxis, the Review Stars entry. This row shows us possible explanatory variables or good features we can incorporate in the next step of our predictive analytics task, in which we predict the star category given a Yelper's review. Below, we see in the table that review stars is correlated highly with the average business star, the reviewer's average star given, negative to positive word ratio, and negative and positive word rates. The first two factors are a helpful giveaway since they are averages of review stars. However, we chose not to use them so that our classifier model is robust in applications where average business star or the user's average star is unknown. Finally, positive and negative word rates are good indicators and correlate positively and negatively accordingly with star category, and we will see later on in the predictive analytics task that the Naive Bayes classifier naturally uses the positive and negative word rate as conditional probabilities/relative frequencies in its calculation. Machine Learning Mission: Predictive Analytics

We split the TURBO data into training and testing subsets starting with a 60-40 ratio like in Andrew Ng's online Machine Learning class, and then applied several different multi-class classifiers in sklearn to compare their accuracy.

In the first round we tried Bag of Words Multinomial NB (Naive Bayes), Bernoulli NB, Bigram Multinomial NB, Trigram Multinomial NB, Bigram-Trigram Multinomial NB, Random Forest with 100 Estimators, and SVC:

Model Recall Mean Precision Mean Recall RSS Precision RSS
Bag of Words Multinomial NB 0.62 0.62 0.21 0.03
Bernoulli NB 0.55 0.55 0.30 0.07
Bigram Multinomial NB 0.66 0.68 0.24 0.04
Trigram Multinomial NB 0.64 0.67 0.28 0.05
Bigram-Trigram Multinomial NB 0.67 0.70 0.26 0.07
Random Forest (100 Estimators) 0.68 0.70 0.23 0.04
SVC 0.52 0.54 0.51 0.03

As a short explanation, precision and recall are performance metrics of the different classification models, representing "quality" and "quantity" respectively of documents classified, and ranging between 0 and 1 inclusive.

Let's look into the confusion matrices to see why RSS is necessary:



Precision and recall RSS are necessary because, for example, in one extreme the SVC classifier attempts to classify almost all reviews as 5 stars to maximize accuracy, given that the majority of reviews are 5 stars. The recall and precision RSS values would be high because 5 star reviews would be far more accurate than 1, 2, 3 and 4 star reviews. However, this short-sighted favoritism towards 5 star reviews comes at a cost of higher inaccuracy (lower precision and recall values) for classifying the 1, 2, 3, and 4 star reviews. Even in other classifiers as well, 4 and 5 star reviews are more favored because they represent the majority (nearly 80%) of Yelp reviews, so as a result 1,2, and 3 star reviews are harder to classify with 2 stars being the most difficult!

After analyzing the results of the first round, we decided that the top model, Random Forest with 100 Estimators, takes too long train (approximately 2 minutes) and has varying mean recall values ranging from 68-70% due to its nondeterministic algorithm. In addition, the next best model, the bigram-trigram Multinomial Naive Bayes, only performs 1-2% better than model number 3, the Bigram Multinomial, so we decided in the next round to improve the performance of Bigram Multinomial Naive Bayes and hopefully tackle the skewed classes situation.

We searched online on how to improve the Naive Bayes classifier for datasets with skewed class distributions and tried implementing the strategy of E Frank, RR Bouckaert (2006) to intialize the word count priors (Laplacian smoothing parameter) with a normalized value as well another strategy by Rennie J., Shih, L., Teevan, J., & Karger, D. (2003) to use the relative word count of complement classes as the conditional probability parameter, both in the attempt to improve Naive Bayes' shortcoming with skewed classes.

The idea of the Naive Bayes algorithm is meant to be simple in its assumptions and probability calculations, but for those who are not math-inclined we have hidden it from sight. Otherwise, click here or the button below in order to show our mathematical explanation if you're curious about it or wish to review/reaffirm what you may already know in your heart!

We wrote our own version of Naive Bayes included OvA and Complement support, and made sure to use vectorization in our code with numpy for efficiency.

Also, utilizing the factor analysis information we gathered the EDA phase and the fact that the negative to positive word count ratio was a highly correlated factor (ρ ≅ -0.35) excluding business stars and the reviewer's average stars (both of which we assume are unknown to model realistic scenarios), we also attempted feature engineering by increasing the number of negative sentiment words (obtained from Bing Liu's opinion mining database) found in Yelp reviews. This model is what we dub the "Overly Negative" Naive Bayes in the effort to better discriminate 1, 2, and 3 star reviews from being confused with 4 star reviews. Just out of curiosity we also played with sklearn's Multiclass Logistic Regression model an also trained it on the same design matrix as the Overly Naive Bayes model. All these models in the second round used bigrams as the tokens:


Model Recall Mean Precision Mean Recall RSS Precision RSS
Complement NB 0.66 0.67 0.24 0.04
One-versus-All NB 0.66 0.67 0.22 0.03
Overly Negative NB 0.65 0.64 0.19 0.01
Multiclass Logistic Regression 0.67 0.68 0.22 0.01

In comparison to the models in the first round, particularly the Bigram Multinomial Naive Bayes, the recall and precision averages of the second round models did not increase, but their recall and precision RSS decreased slightly. It seemed that even though we put so much effort in feature engineering and taking the sentiment of the Yelp reviews into account, our models still could not surpass the 68-70% recall and precision results. At this point, we realized two things: that we may have met a recall and precision accuracy barrier/upper bound given the size of our training data, but that we could also try optimizing the RSS values if not the average recall and precision. Knowing this we attempted a strategy called oversampling in order to combat the unfairly skewed accuracy to the 4 and 5 star reviews because of their 80% majority and in the effort to improve the recall and precisions of the 1, 2, and 3 star reviews (the goal of which is the same as decreasing the RSS). Observing the fact that 1, 2, and 3 star reviews are commonly misclassified as 4 star reviews, we used oversampling on the 60% training data to replicate the number of 1, 2, and 3 star reviews until they each were equal to the amount of 4 star reviews.

As the third and final round of our machine learning mission, the result of Bigram Multinomial with oversampling for 1, 2, and 3 star reviews compared with the non-oversampled Bigram Multinomial from the first round is optimistic:


Model Recall Mean Precision Mean Recall RSS Precision RSS
Bigram Multinomial NB with Oversampling for Minority Classes 0.66 0.65 0.11 0.07
Bigram Multinomial NB from Round 1 without Oversampling 0.66 0.68 0.24 0.04

As a small tradeoff for "quality" of Yelp reviews classified, represented by the precision score, we gained a drastically reduced recall RSS score which means that the the "quantity" of Yelp reviews classified correctly for each star category became fairer. This is an improvement from the previous Bigram Multinomial Naive Bayes model without oversampling in round 1, where 4 and 5 star Yelp reviews had a higher quantity classified correctly at a cost of the recall for 1, 2, and 3 star reviews. As you can see in the below confusion matrices for the third round, the diagonals for the Oversampled Bigram Naive Bayes model have brighter values for the 1, 2, and 3 star reviews than the non-oversampled model:


To conclude our machine learning mission, we varied the training-testing dataset sizes to observe the result that as training data increased, our Naive Bayes classifiers made less errors on less testing data and therefore recall and precision scores increased:

Model Recall Mean Precision Mean Recall RSS Precision RSS
Bigram Multinomial NB with Oversampling and 80% Training 0.68 0.68 0.10 0.06
Bigram Multinomial NB with Oversampling and 60% Training 0.66 0.68 0.11 0.06
Bigram Multinomial NB with Oversampling and 40% Training 0.63 0.63 0.12 0.08
Bigram Multinomial NB with Oversampling and 20% Training 0.57 0.56 0.14 0.10

In comparison with Random Forest with 100 Learners on various training data sizes and no oversampling, although Bigram Naive Bayes with oversampling is only around 2-3% less in recall and less 5-7% in precision, it makes up by being less biased in classifying the 4 and 5 star Yelp reviews by 10-16% as shown by the recall RSS! This is a tradeoff worth considering, since 4 and 5 star reviews make up the 80% majority and we'd like to focus on improving the recall and precision of the 1, 2, and 3 star Yelp reviews even though they each only comprise less than 15% of the total Yelp reviews in our sample.

Model Recall Mean Precision Mean Recall RSS Precision RSS
Random Forest (100 Learners) w/o Oversampling and 80% Training 0.71 0.73 0.20 0.03
Random Forest (100 Learners) w/o Oversampling and 60% Training 0.68 0.70 0.23 0.04
Random Forest (100 Learners) w/o Oversampling and 40% Training 0.65 0.68 0.26 0.05
Random Forest (100 Learners) w/o Oversampling and 20% Training 0.60 0.63 0.30 0.06

Finally in terms of classifier accuracy, again comparing the Bigram Multinomial NB with oversampling to Random Forest with 100 Learners, not only is the NB model quicker to train (the time ranging in seconds) while Random Forest takes a few minutes (and it is also a nondeterminstic algorithm), the recall scores that represent the quantity of Yelp reviews classified correctly is only 2-3% less, suggesting that a simple classifier like NB performs close to optimal on par with a more complicated ensemble model like Random Forest. A classic explanation that convinced us our Bigram Naive Bayes with oversampling results were on the right level of accuracy with Random Forest is an interesting paper by Zhang, H. (2004) that describes the optimal performance behind Naive Bayes.
